Trust & Security

Last updated: July 3, 2026 · entity: AiTrade · every claim on this page is architectural or verifiable — nothing here is a badge we haven't earned.

RailCall is local-first by architecture, not by policy. Your business data never leaves your machine because there is nowhere on our side for it to go — execution runs on 127.0.0.1, and keys live in a 0600 vault on your own disk. Outbound is limited to the blind billing pulse (SHA-256 key hash + nonce + action) and — on hosted builds — the plain-English description you type (zero-retention). Your records, provider keys, and receipts never leave your machine. That architecture answers most of a security questionnaire definitionally, before any promise. Below is the honest, precise version — including exactly what does leave the machine, because a trust page that hides its egress isn't one.

1 · Architecture by design

A cloud automation SaaS sits between your systems — your records, payment events, and OAuth tokens land in their database. RailCall inverts that. The Studio and the governed runtime execute entirely on the operator's machine, bound to the loopback interface (127.0.0.1). Provider keys are read from a local, owner-only (0600) vault and used in-process; they are never transmitted to us. Data is read from one connector, handed to the next, and dropped. The only thing that persists is the signed proof it happened — on your disk, in your control.

The strongest true statements we can make — that a cloud SaaS structurally cannot:
  • Your business data never leaves your machine — we are architecturally incapable of receiving it.
  • A breach of our infrastructure cannot expose data that was never transmitted to us.
  • Absolute data residency: processing is pinned to your own hardware, not a region we promise.
  • Offline-verifiable proof: signed receipts on your disk verify without ever contacting us.

1.1 · The operator trust boundary stated plainly

RailCall is a single-operator, local application. The trust boundary is the operator's own machine. Being explicit about what that does and does not cover is part of the posture — a buyer's compliance team will ask, so we answer first.

What the hard floors, airlock, and dry-run do protect against — structurally, on every run:
  • A misbehaving or prompt-injected agent. No agent can fire an irreversible action, an unclassified action, a policy change, or unpriced spend — all four are held for a human, no rule can override, every decision is receipted.
  • Accidental sends. Execution is dry-run by default; nothing crosses the wire until a human approves it in the terminal.
  • Silent policy drift. Every policy version is signed and hash-chained — a change is a reviewable, attributable diff, not a quiet mutation.
  • Tampering after the fact. Receipts are Ed25519-signed and chained; editing history breaks the chain, so operator tampering is tamper-evident, verifiable offline from disk.

What it does not claim. The floors protect against bad agents, not a malicious or root-compromised operator on their own machine. Anyone with root on a box can edit any local file — that is true of every local developer tool, and it is outside the boundary a local runtime can enforce. What we give you against that is tamper-evidence (edits surface as broken receipt chains), not tamper-proofing. For teams that need a second set of eyes, the mitigation is centralized receipt review (§1.2) and, on the roadmap, independent attestation (§10) — never a claim that the local box is unbreakable.

1.2 · Adopting RailCall across a team local today · shared review on roadmap

A dev shop running client work does not want fifty people each improvising. RailCall is built so a team can standardize without pooling secrets:

  • Keys stay local, per operator (BYOK). Each person's provider keys live in their own 0600 vault and never leave their machine — there is no shared key store to breach, and no raw key ever transits RailCall.
  • Workflows and policies are portable artifacts, not secrets. A composed rail and its policy (hard floors, auto-approve rules, blast-radius limits) are signed, versioned files. Author an internal standard workflow once, share the file, and every operator runs the identical governed rail — without sharing a single credential.
  • Receipts export for central review. The signed, hash-chained ledger exports so a lead or compliance reviewer can verify — offline, from disk — what every operator's agents actually did, and that no floor was bypassed.
  • What is still single-machine today. A hosted shared receipt ledger and org-level SSO / SCIM / role management live on the hosted control plane and are being built out (§7). We will not describe them as shipped local features until they are.

2 · What leaves your machine — precisely disclosed

Three things can cross your network boundary. We name all three, because the honesty is the point.

🔒
Your business data — never leaves, on any path
Customer records, PII, payment events, ticket bodies, files, connector payloads. Read from one system, handed to the next, dropped. Never transits RailCall's cloud. There is no RailCall data store holding it.
✍️
Your build prompt — the plain-English description you type
Hosted engine (default): the description is proxied through our gateway to the model provider (Groq) to compose the workflow — zero-retention: never persisted, never logged. BYOK: the description goes straight from your machine to your own model provider — we never see it at all. Either way, your business data is never part of this; only the workflow description is.
📊
A blind billing ping
To meter usage: the SHA-256 hash of your API key + a one-time nonce + the action. No customer data, schema, prompts, outputs, or logs — it is a metering register, not a data sink. You can confirm this on the wire (Wireshark) and in every receipt as external_sockets: 0 during compute.

We will not claim "zero data ever leaves." That would be false the moment you use the hosted build engine or we meter a flow. What is true — and stronger — is that your business data never leaves, the build prompt is zero-retention (or fully local with BYOK), and billing is blind. Precision beats a slogan.

4 · Data handling local-first

What we hold on our side is limited to what running an account requires: your email, the SHA-256 hash of your API key (never the raw key at rest), and your flow balance / usage counters. That's it. We do not receive, store, or process your customer records, connector payloads, prompts (BYOK), outputs, schemas, or files. We do not sell personal information, and we do not use your operational or work content to train models.

5 · Encryption in place

  • In transit: TLS 1.2+ on the billing ping and account API. The hosted build proxy uses TLS to the model provider.
  • At rest (your side): provider keys and the Ed25519 signing seed live in a local 0600 owner-only vault on your disk — you custody them; we never hold them. Protect them with your OS disk encryption (FileVault / LUKS), same as ~/.ssh.
  • At rest (our side): the account database (email, key-hash, balance) is encrypted at rest by the managed database provider.

6 · Residency, retention & deletion you control it

Residency: your data is processed on your own hardware, wherever that is — the strongest possible residency guarantee. Retention: we retain no customer data, so there is none on our side to retain or leak; your receipts and policy chain live on your disk under your control (back them up with railcall backup). Deletion: request deletion via railcall.ai/discord and we remove your email + key-hash + counters; your local artifacts are yours to keep or delete.

7 · Access control & authentication SSO on roadmap

Account auth uses PBKDF2-hashed passwords (per-user salt, constant-time verify). The workspace supports organizations, members, and role-based access; the local Studio binds every mutating endpoint to a per-launch session token, so a random web page cannot drive it. Because the runtime is self-hosted and loopback-only, the first line of access control is your own machine's OS boundary.

SSO / SAML, SCIM & MFA: enterprise roadmap — not shipped today, said plainly. We know a big-co identity team treats SAML, SCIM (automated de-provisioning), and MFA as pass/fail. None of them are built yet. When they ship, basic SAML will not sit behind a premium "SSO tax." For a supervised pilot on your own infrastructure, ask us — we'll scope it with your security team.

9 · AI-specific controls built

  • No training on your content. With BYOK, your prompts go to your provider under your no-train / zero-retention terms — we never intermediate them. The hosted engine is proxy-only, zero-retention.
  • Agent identity & attribution. Every governed action is attributed in a signed receipt to a human approval or a named policy version (policy_auto (v2)) — never an anonymous agent.
  • Kill switch & hard floors. Execution is dry-run + approval-gated by default. Four hard floors that no agent and no policy rule can override always hold irreversible actions, unclassified actions, policy changes, and unpriced spend for a human — and every decision is signed into a tamper-evident receipt (operator edits break the chain, see §1.1). One click reverts policy to strictest.
  • Reversibility classification. Every action is classed reversible / compensable / irreversible, and irreversible actions can never auto-fire — a control the major agent platforms don't expose.

10 · Testing & responsible disclosure honest

Today: structural self-audit + a deterministic gate suite (creds-before-socket, plan-purity, rollback, injection-sanitization) enforced per connector, plus the tamper-evidence built into every receipt. Not yet: an independent third-party penetration test — it's planned before serious enterprise rollout, and we won't imply one exists until it does.

Responsible disclosure: found something? Ping us on Discord — railcall.ai/discord. We'll acknowledge, work it with you, and credit you if you'd like. Please don't run automated attacks against the shared gateway.

11 · Incident response documented

We maintain a written incident-response process: detect → contain → assess → notify affected account holders without undue delay → remediate → post-mortem. Because we hold no customer data, the blast radius of any incident on our side is limited to account metadata (email, key-hash, balance) — your business data was never on our side to be exposed.
